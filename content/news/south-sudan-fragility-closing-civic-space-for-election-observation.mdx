# **Civic Space Status:** Closed

South Sudan represents one of the most constrained civic environments for citizen election observation in Africa, characterized not only by legal and administrative limitations but by **deep structural fragility and weak state capacity**. The study places South Sudan within the **closed to highly restricted category across several dimensions**, reflecting an environment where meaningful citizen observation is largely absent. 

At the regulatory level, there is **no well-established or functional legal framework** that effectively supports citizen election observation. While civil society exists, the absence of clear, enforceable provisions for observer accreditation and protection limits their ability to operate independently. 

Administratively, South Sudan faces **severe institutional capacity gaps**, with weak or underdeveloped systems for accrediting and coordinating citizen observers. This results in limited or inconsistent access to electoral processes, further constraining observer participation. 

The relationship between citizen observers and electoral authorities is **minimal and largely underdeveloped**, with few structured platforms for engagement or consultation. Citizen observers are not fully recognized as key stakeholders in the electoral process, limiting their influence on electoral integrity and reform. 

The most significant constraints are found in **safety and operational conditions**. South Sudan’s volatile security environment, characterized by ongoing conflict and militarization of public space, severely restricts observer mobility, deployment, and independence. These conditions make nationwide observation extremely difficult and, in some cases, unfeasible. 

Access to election data and information is **extremely limited**, with weak infrastructure and lack of transparency further undermining the ability of observers to conduct credible monitoring. Funding environments are also highly constrained, with **most financial support channelled through international organizations**, leaving local observer groups dependent on external intermediaries and limiting their autonomy. 

Public perception of citizen observers is **low and underdeveloped**, reflecting both limited visibility of observer organizations and broader institutional weaknesses. Post-election dialogue mechanisms are virtually absent, with little opportunity for observer findings to inform electoral reforms. 

**Strategic Insight:** South Sudan demonstrates that state fragility can be as limiting as direct repression in shrinking civic space. Without foundational investments in security, institutional capacity, and legal frameworks, citizen election observation cannot function effectively. Strengthening civic space in such contexts requires a holistic approach that goes beyond legal reform to include peacebuilding, institutional development, and support for local civil society.












## **Recommendations**

*  **Establish a legal framework recognizing citizen observers**
*  **Build foundational CSO capacity for election monitoring**
*  **Channel funding directly to local organizations where feasible**
*  **Integrate observer protection into peacebuilding and governance programs**
