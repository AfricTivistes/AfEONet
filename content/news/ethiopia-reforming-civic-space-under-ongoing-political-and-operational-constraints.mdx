# **Civic Space Status:** Restricted

Ethiopia reflects a civic space in transition, where recent legal reforms have expanded opportunities for citizen election observation, yet **significant political, regulatory, and operational constraints continue to limit effectiveness**. The study places Ethiopia within the **restricted category**, indicating that while civic participation is legally permitted, it is shaped by conditional freedoms and uneven implementation. 

At the regulatory level, the introduction of the **Civil Society Proclamation (No. 1113/2019)** marked a notable shift from earlier restrictive frameworks, easing some limitations on civil society operations and allowing greater engagement in election observation. However, the law retains **key constraints**, including requirements that organizations mobilize a portion of their funding from domestic sources, which limits flexibility and sustainability for observer groups. 

Administratively, Ethiopia performs relatively strongly in accreditation, with the study noting **high scores (up to 10/10)** for administrative access in certain contexts. Accreditation processes are generally structured and allow observer participation across key stages of the electoral process. However, implementation remains uneven, particularly in regions affected by conflict or political instability. 

The relationship between citizen observers and the Electoral Management Body is **formally recognized and relatively functional**, with engagement mechanisms in place. Nevertheless, this relationship is influenced by broader political dynamics, and observer influence on electoral reforms remains limited. 

The most significant constraints emerge in **safety and operational space**, particularly in conflict-affected regions such as Tigray and Oromia. Observers face **security risks, restricted mobility, and heightened surveillance**, which limit nationwide deployment and independent monitoring. 

Access to election data and information is **partially available**, though inconsistencies in data dissemination and infrastructure gaps affect timely analysis. Funding environments are also constrained, with regulatory requirements and political sensitivities shaping how resources are accessed and utilized. 

Public perception of citizen observers is generally **mixed**, with recognition of their role in promoting transparency, but also skepticism in politically polarized contexts. Post-election dialogue exists but is not consistently institutionalized, limiting the uptake of observer recommendations. 

**Strategic Insight:** Ethiopia demonstrates that legal reform, while essential, is insufficient on its own to guarantee an enabling civic space. Sustained progress will depend on aligning regulatory improvements with political openness, strengthening security conditions, and enhancing the independence and operational capacity of citizen observer organizations.












## **Recommendations**

*  **Remove restrictive domestic funding requirements for CSOs**
*  **Strengthen legal safeguards against surveillance and intimidation**
* **Improve access to electoral data and observer deployment in conflict areas**
* **Enhance EMB transparency and stakeholder engagement**
