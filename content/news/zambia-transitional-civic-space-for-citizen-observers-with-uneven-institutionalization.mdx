## **Civic Space Status:** Restricted

Nigeria presents a complex and evolving civic space for citizen election observation, characterized by notable progress in transparency alongside persistent structural and regulatory constraints. Under the study’s framework, Nigeria falls within the **restricted category**, where legal protections exist but are shaped by compliance burdens and political sensitivities

A defining strength is Nigeria’s leadership in **electoral data transparency**, particularly through innovations such as the INEC Result Viewing Portal (IReV), which enables near real-time access to polling station–level results. This has significantly enhanced public scrutiny, empowered observer verification, and positioned Nigeria as a continental reference point for open electoral data systems. 

Administratively, observer accreditation is generally accessible, but the broader regulatory environment imposes **significant compliance demands**, including stringent financial reporting requirements and scrutiny of donor funding under legal frameworks such as anti-money laundering provisions. These measures, while framed as accountability mechanisms, often create operational friction for observer organizations.

The relationship between citizen observers and the Independent National Electoral Commission (INEC) is **formalized but uneven in practice**. While leading domestic groups are accredited and engaged, observer feedback—particularly on sensitive electoral integrity issues—is not always addressed in a timely or transparent manner, limiting the influence of observation on reform processes. 

In terms of **safety and operational conditions**, Nigeria reflects an obstructed environment, with observers facing logistical barriers, restricted access to some collation centers, and security risks in politically volatile or conflict-affected regions such as parts of Lagos, Rivers, and the North-East. 

Access to funding is relatively open compared to more restrictive contexts, allowing observer organizations to mobilize international support. However, **regulatory oversight and compliance pressures** continue to shape how funds are accessed and utilized. 

Finally, while citizen observers are widely recognized and active, **post-election dialogue and uptake of recommendations remain limited**, constraining the long-term impact of observation efforts on electoral reform. 

**Implication:** Nigeria demonstrates that advances in electoral transparency—particularly through digital innovation—can significantly expand civic space. However, sustaining this progress will require stronger institutional responsiveness, reduced regulatory friction, and more structured engagement between EMBs and citizen observers across the electoral cycle.

## **Recommendations**

* Codify real-time results transparency into electoral law
* Simplify CSO compliance and reporting frameworks
* Strengthen feedback mechanisms between INEC and observer groups
* Expand observer access to collation centers and backend systems
